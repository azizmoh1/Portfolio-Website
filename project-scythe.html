Overview

Introduction

This project required designing a decorative cantilever traffic signal pole for Chicago intersections, commemorating the centennial of the 1933 World's Fair. The pole needed to span a 26-foot road, support three 60-pound luminaires and three traffic signs, and survive wind speeds exceeding 95.7 mph — all while maintaining 18 feet of clearance and a factor of safety above 2.

The final design is called "The Scythe" — named for its sweeping curved arm that arcs out from the pole and holds all six required fixtures. The goal was to meet every constraint without overcomplicating the design. Efficiency and reliability were the priority, but aesthetics still mattered given the World's Fair context and the public-facing nature of the structure.

Requirements

Design Objectives

Structural Constraints

  • Span 26-foot road clearance
  • 18 ft minimum overhead clearance
  • Factor of safety ≥ 2 under all loads
  • Survive 95.7 mph wind loading
  • Support 3 × 60 lb luminaires
  • Support 3 × 5 lb traffic signs

Design Goals

  • Cost-effective material selection
  • Durable in Chicago climate conditions
  • Lightweight and material-optimized
  • Aesthetically fitting for a landmark city
  • Environmentally considerate
  • Manufacturable and installable

Ideation

Design Process & Iterations

Three Initial Concepts (4/6)

Three concepts were sketched on April 6th. Sketch 1 was a symmetric truss with hanging luminaires and a wide flared base — wind load complications and excess material ruled it out. Sketch 3 was efficient but too generic, similar to standard municipal poles. Sketch 2 — a curved arch mast arm — was selected for its balance of structural promise and visual distinctiveness fitting of the World's Fair context.

Revision 1 (4/22) — Sketch 2 Improved

The first SolidWorks build proved overly complex and material-heavy. Peer review flagged excess weight and wasted geometry. A revised version was developed with specific improvements: reduce overall material, use more effective material placement, incorporate four reinforcing plates at stress concentration zones, and add two wiring cutouts in the pole for electrical access. Dimensions were formalized — pole standing ~24 ft tall with a square base plate for four anchor bolts.

Revision 2 (4/23) — New Curve Geometry

The previous arch geometry couldn't be translated cleanly into SolidWorks without errors. Rather than continuing to patch a flawed foundation, the geometry was scrapped entirely in favor of a simpler single-curve form — a sweeping arc rising from the pole and curving outward over the road. This became the scythe shape that carried through to the final design after four additional CAD revisions refining the lattice members and resolving rebuild issues.

Final Model

Final Design

The final model features a vertical pole with a square base plate designed for four anchor bolts, two wiring cutouts along the pole body, and a latticed mast arm composed of curved members connected by cross bracing. The lattice configuration distributes load along the arm effectively while giving the structure its distinctive scythe-like appearance. All six required fixtures — three luminaires and three signs — are mounted along the arm and pole as specified.

Analysis

Finite Element Analysis

Setup & Boundary Conditions

Static structural analysis was performed in ANSYS Mechanical 2025 R1. The geometry was imported as a STEP file from SolidWorks with structural steel assigned as the material. A fixed support was applied at the base of the pole simulating the anchor bolt connection to the cast-in-place concrete pedestal.

Loads applied: standard earth gravity, three luminaire point forces at 60 lbs each, three sign forces at 5 lbs each, wind pressure calculated at 95.7 mph, and additional mounting point forces — totaling seven force inputs alongside the applied pressure.

Mesh

Tet10 tetrahedral elements with adaptive sizing, resolution 4, slow transition, fine span angle center. Bounding box diagonal: 10.27 m. Min edge length: 5.68×10⁻⁵ m. Average surface area: 0.13457 m². Two warnings were noted (model length unit change, potential mesh object loss) — neither affected the validity of results.

Results: The minimum safety factor of 4.6116 occurs at the base of the pole where bending stress is highest — more than double the required minimum of 2. The average safety factor across the structure was 14.955. Maximum deformation of 0.045227 m (45.2 mm) occurs at the tip of the mast arm, within acceptable deflection limits. These results confirm the design meets all structural requirements under the specified loading conditions.

Materials

Material Selection & Cost

✓ Stainless Steel (Selected)

Cost: $11,885 · Weight: 8.985 metric tons

Lower cost, superior corrosion resistance for Chicago's harsh climate, durability score of 8. Better long-term value for a municipal infrastructure application.

Aluminum (Evaluated)

Cost: $17,026 · Weight: 3.090 metric tons

~65% lighter but $5,141 more expensive. Both materials produced an identical design metric score of 2 — no clear structural advantage justified the cost premium.

Takeaways

Challenges & Reflection

The biggest challenge was CAD modeling — translating sketch concepts into clean, error-free SolidWorks geometry took far more iteration than expected. The original arch design created intersecting surfaces that couldn't be properly constrained. The right call was to simplify the geometry entirely rather than keep patching a flawed foundation, which produced a cleaner, lighter, and structurally superior final design.

Balancing material reduction with structural performance was an ongoing challenge throughout. Early iterations were heavier than necessary, but cutting too much risked dropping below the safety factor threshold. This was managed through iterative FEA runs — trimming geometry where safety factors were highest and preserving material where stresses were concentrated, particularly at the base of the pole.

The project reinforced a core engineering lesson: what looks simple on paper often requires multiple complete restarts before arriving at something workable. The iterative nature of design isn't a sign of failure — it's the process.
